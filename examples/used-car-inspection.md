# Example: Inspecting a Used Car End-to-End

**Skill:** [Used Car Inspector](../skills/used-car-inspector/)
**Scenario:** A buyer evaluates a private-party listing — from pre-contact research through inspection findings to a negotiation position.

---

**User:**

> I'm considering this listing: 2014 BMW 328i (F30), 95,000 miles, private seller, asking $11,900. "Well maintained, records available, minor cosmetic wear." I've never owned a European car. What should I know before I even contact them?

> 💡 *Starting before contact is the skill's recommended workflow — model-specific research shapes every later question.*

**Assistant:**

The F30 328i uses the N20 turbo four — a good engine with well-documented weak points you must check at this mileage:

1. **Timing chain and oil pump drive chain** — early N20s (before ~2015 production) had chain guide failures; catastrophic if it lets go. Ask whether the chain/guides were ever replaced and listen for cold-start rattle.
2. **Oil filter housing gasket and valve cover gasket leaks** — near-universal by 95k. Budget $400–800 at an independent shop if leaking.
3. **Electric water pump** — typical life 60–90k miles; ~$700–900 replaced. Ask if it's been done.
4. **Turbo wastegate rattle** and **charge pipe** (plastic, cracks under boost).

Questions to ask remotely: service history at BMW or independent (records!), which of the above items are documented as done, any warning lights, and why they're selling. On price: clean examples with records run $10,500–13,000 in most US markets, so the ask is plausible *if* the chain-era work and water pump are documented.

**User:**

> Seller says: oil changes every 7-8k with receipts, water pump done at 78k, no knowledge of timing chain work, "no leaks that he knows of." Viewing is Saturday. Give me a checklist ordered so deal-breakers come first.

**Assistant:**

Deal-breaker-first checklist for this specific car:

**First 10 minutes (walk away if these fail):**
1. **Cold start** — insist the engine is stone cold when you arrive. Listen for 2–3 seconds of rattle at startup (chain guides) — this is the single most important test on this car.
2. **VIN match** — title, dash, door jamb all agree; title is clean, seller's name is on it.
3. **Warning lights** — all should illuminate with ignition on, all should extinguish after start.

**Engine bay (next 10 minutes):**
4. Oil filter housing area (front-left of block) — look for wet, dark seepage running down.
5. Valve cover edges and spark plug wells — same.
6. Coolant level and color; any crusty residue at hose joints.
7. Oil cap and dipstick — milky residue means head gasket concerns; walk away.

**Test drive (must include):**
8. Full-throttle pull once warm — hesitation or boost whoosh = charge pipe/wastegate.
9. Steady 60 mph — vibration check; F30s eat front control arm bushings.
10. Braking from speed — shudder = warped rotors (minor) but note it for negotiation.

Photograph everything questionable. Report back before you make any offer.

**User:**

> Back from the viewing. Cold start was clean, no rattle. But: (1) oil filter housing has wet oil residue and it's dripped onto the belt area, (2) both front tires are at the wear bars, (3) slight steering wheel vibration at 65 mph, (4) records confirm the water pump but oil changes were DIY after 60k with receipts for parts only.

> 💡 *Specific findings, not impressions — this is what lets the assistant price the negotiation accurately.*

**Assistant:**

No deal-breakers, but real money on the table. Pricing the findings at independent-shop rates:

| Finding | Likely fix | Cost estimate |
|---|---|---|
| Oil filter housing gasket leak (oil on belt = do it soon; oil-soaked belts fail) | Gasket + likely belt | $500–800 |
| Two front tires at wear bars | Pair of quality tires + alignment | $450–600 |
| Vibration at 65 mph | Could be just the worn tires; possibly control arm bushings | $0–600 (unknown) |
| DIY oil changes, parts receipts only | Not a defect, but weakens the "well maintained" premium | leverage |

Concrete, defensible need: roughly **$1,000–1,400**, plus unquantified bushing risk. A fair counter is **$10,300–10,600**, presented as: "I like the car; here's the shop math on the leak and tires." If he won't move below $11,500, a $150 pre-purchase inspection at a BMW independent is worth it before walking — it converts the vibration unknown into either leverage or peace of mind.

**User:**

> Offered $10,400 with the itemized list, settled at $10,700 with him throwing in the winter wheel set. PPI booked before final payment.

> 💡 *The PPI before money changes hands is the last safety net the skill insists on — especially wise on a first European car.*

---

## Takeaways

- Model-specific research (N20 chain, oil housing, water pump) turned a generic viewing into a targeted inspection.
- Ordering the checklist deal-breakers-first respects everyone's time.
- Findings were converted to **shop-rate numbers**, which is what makes a negotiation position credible.
- The buyer never skipped the professional PPI despite a good inspection — layers, not shortcuts.
